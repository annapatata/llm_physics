## 0. What This Paper Is About

A transformer (GPT-2 small) is trained from scratch on synthetic strings generated by context-free grammars (CFGs). The CFGs are designed to be hard — locally ambiguous, requiring dynamic programming (DP) to parse. The paper then asks: after training on next-token prediction alone, does the model internally encode the parse tree structure? And do its attention patterns resemble DP information flow?

**The answer to both is yes.** This is the core finding.

The paper has three parts:
1. **Results 1-3:** Can GPT learn these CFGs? (generation accuracy, diversity, KL divergence)
2. **Results 4-5:** Do hidden states linearly encode parse tree structure? (probing)
3. **Results 6-9:** Do attention patterns resemble DP? (attention analysis)

## 1. The CFGs

### Structure

All CFGs used in the main paper are **L=7 level layered CFGs**:
- Level 1: single root symbol
- Levels 2-6: nonterminal symbols (NTs)
- Level 7: terminal symbols (Ts) — these are the actual tokens the model sees

Rules expand one symbol at level ℓ into 2 or 3 children at level ℓ+1. Generation is top-down: start from root, randomly pick a rule at each step, expand until level 7. The flat sequence of level-7 terminals is what the model receives as input.

### The Five Hard CFGs

All have size `(1, 3, 3, 3, 3, 3, 3)` — only 3 nonterminals per level. This creates maximum ambiguity — any token could plausibly belong to multiple subtrees. Parsing requires global DP.

| Dataset | Rule degree \|R(a)\| | Special property | Difficulty |
|---------|---------------------|-----------------|------------|
| cfg3b | 2 | consecutive symbols forced distinct | easiest hard |
| cfg3i | 2 | distinctness removed | — |
| cfg3h | 2 or 3 | — | — |
| cfg3g | 3 | — | — |
| cfg3f | 3 or 4 | — | hardest; at GPT2-small limit |

String lengths: 25th-95th percentile roughly 200-370 tokens. Maximum possible = 3^6 = 729.

Two easy CFGs also used: cfg3e1 `(1,3,9,27,81,27,9)` and cfg3e2 `(1,3,9,27,27,9,4)` — many symbols per level, low ambiguity, trivially greedily parseable.

### Key Notation

For any generated string x with its parse tree:

- **s_ℓ(i)** — ancestor symbol: which nonterminal at level ℓ generated token at position i. This is the node's label.
- **p_ℓ(i)** — ancestor index: which numbered subtree (counting left to right, starting at 1) does token i belong to at level ℓ.
- **b_ℓ(i)** — boundary indicator: equals 1 if token i is the LAST token of its level-ℓ subtree. Formally: b_ℓ(i) = 1 iff p_ℓ(i) ≠ p_ℓ(i+1), or i = len(x).
- **b♯(i)** — deepest NT-end: the smallest ℓ ∈ {2,...,L-1} such that b_ℓ(i)=1. The finest-grained boundary this position sits on.

These are computed during generation and stored alongside each string. They are the ground truth labels for probing experiments.

**Why p_ℓ matters:** two tokens can have the same ancestor symbol (same nonterminal label) but different ancestor indices (different occurrences of that nonterminal). p_ℓ disambiguates them. Boundaries are defined using p_ℓ, not s_ℓ.

## 2. The Models

### Primary Models

**GPT (absolute):** Standard GPT-2 small architecture. 12 layers, 12 heads, 768 dimensions (=12×64), 86M parameters. Absolute positional embeddings. This is the baseline that FAILS on hard CFGs.

**GPT_rel:** GPT-2 with relative positional attention from DeBERTa. The attention score between positions j and i includes a learned embedding for the relative distance |j-i|. More expressive but slower to train.

**GPT_rot:** GPT-2 with rotary positional embeddings (RoPE). Queries and keys are rotated by an angle proportional to their position. The dot product Q·K then naturally depends only on position difference j-i, not absolute positions. Uses GPT-NeoX implementation. Slightly less powerful than GPT_rel but much faster.

**GPT_pos:** Like GPT_rel but removes all content-dependent terms from attention. Attention depends only on relative position, not on what the tokens actually are. Tests how much structure can be learned from position alone.

**GPT_uni:** No learned attention at all. 8 heads, each head h uniformly averages the previous 2^(h-1) tokens:
- Head 1: average of previous 1 token
- Head 2: average of previous 2 tokens
- Head 3: average of previous 4 tokens
- Head 4: average of previous 8 tokens
- Head 5: average of previous 16 tokens
- Head 6: average of previous 32 tokens
- Head 7: average of previous 64 tokens
- Head 8: average of previous 128 tokens

No Q, K matrices. Weights are fixed, never updated. All learning happens in feed-forward layers. Uses 12 layers, 1024 dimensions (not 768) to roughly match parameter count with other models.

### Key Finding About Models

GPT_uni beats vanilla GPT (absolute) on hard CFGs. A model with NO learned attention outperforms a model with wrongly-anchored absolute position attention. This is because CFG structure is relative (subtree spans repeat at the same depth regardless of absolute position), and absolute embeddings encode the wrong coordinate system.

### Vocabulary

The vocabulary is tiny: {1, 2, 3} for terminals plus BOS and EOS tokens. Total vocab size = 5. This is NOT the standard GPT-2 50K vocabulary. You are training from scratch on CFG data.

### Training Details (from Appendix A.3)

- Optimizer: AdamW, β=(0.9, 0.98), weight decay=0.1
- Learning rate: 3e-4, linear decay to 0 over 100K steps, NO warmup
- Batch size: 96
- Sequence length: 512 (concatenate strings with BOS/EOS, cut into 512-token windows)
- Total tokens: 4.9 billion (96 × 512 × 100K)
- All fresh samples — regenerate from CFG on the fly, never reuse
- Hardware used in paper: 4× A100 GPUs, less than 1 day per model

## 3. The DP Algorithms

### Why DP Is Needed

Two places DP is needed:
1. **Membership check (CYK):** After GPT generates a string, verify it belongs to L(G). This is O(n³) for length-2 rules after binarization.
2. **Next-token probabilities (inside algorithm):** Compute exact Pr[next token | prefix] under the true CFG distribution, for KL divergence calculation.

### Binarization (Required Preprocessing)

The paper's CFGs have rules of length 2 AND 3. CYK is O(n³) for length-2 rules but O(n⁴) for length-3 rules naively. Fix by converting every length-3 rule A → B C D into:

```
A → B X_aux
X_aux → C D
```

where X_aux is a fresh auxiliary nonterminal. Do this binarization once when loading the CFG, before running any DP.

### CYK Algorithm (Membership Check)

```
table[(i, j, A)] = True if nonterminal A can generate string[i..j]

Base case (single tokens, length=1):
    For each position i:
        For each nonterminal A:
            For each rule A → t:
                If t == string[i]: table[(i, i, A)] = True

Recursive case (longer spans, length = 2 to n):
    For each span length l from 2 to n:
        For each start position i:
            j = i + l - 1
            For each nonterminal A:
                For each rule A → B C (after binarization, all rules length 2):
                    For each split point k from i to j-1:
                        If table[(i,k,B)] and table[(k+1,j,C)]:
                            table[(i,j,A)] = True

Answer: table[(0, n-1, root)]
```

### Inside Algorithm (Probabilities)

Same structure as CYK but sum probabilities instead of boolean OR:

```
alpha[(i, j, A)] = probability that A generates string[i..j]

Base case:
    alpha[(i, i, A)] = sum over rules (A → t) of P(A→t) × [t == string[i]]

Recursive case:
    alpha[(i, j, A)] = sum over rules (A → B C) of
                       sum over split points k of
                       P(A→B C) × alpha[(i,k,B)] × alpha[(k+1,j,C)]
```

Since all rules are uniform probability: P(A → any rule) = 1 / |R(A)| where |R(A)| is the number of rules for nonterminal A.

**The total string probability:** alpha[(0, n-1, root)] gives Pr[string x under CFG G].

**Next-token probability:** To get Pr[next token = t | prefix x_1...x_c], compute the inside table for the prefix, then for each possible extension by t, compute how much probability mass is consistent with a valid continuation. This requires combining inside (prefix) with outside (suffix constraints). Full derivation is the inside-outside algorithm.

### Outside Algorithm

```
beta[(i, j, A)] = probability that everything OUTSIDE span (i..j) 
                  was generated correctly, given A is responsible for (i..j)

Base case:
    beta[(0, n-1, root)] = 1

Recursive case (top-down):
    beta[(i, j, B)] = sum over rules (A → B C) of
                      sum over right extensions j' > j of
                      P(A→BC) × beta[(i, j', A)] × alpha[(j+1, j', C)]
                    + sum over rules (A → C B) of
                      sum over left extensions i' < i of
                      P(A→CB) × beta[(i', j, A)] × alpha[(i', i-1, C)]
```

Combining inside and outside: posterior probability of a specific rule firing at a specific location:

```
P(A→BC at split k for span (i,j) | string x) =
    beta[(i,j,A)] × P(A→BC) × alpha[(i,k,B)] × alpha[(k+1,j,C)]
    ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
                         alpha[(0, n-1, root)]
```

## 4. Results 1-3: Generation Quality

### What Is Measured

**Result 1 — Completion Accuracy:**
- Sample fresh string x from CFG
- Give model prefix of length c (c=0 for full generation, c=50 for completion)
- Model generates autoregressively using multinomial sampling at temperature τ=1
- Check if full string (prefix + generated suffix) is valid using CYK
- Repeat 20,000 times, report fraction valid

**Result 2 — Generation Diversity:**
- Entropy estimation: compare model output entropy to true CFG entropy
- Birthday paradox: if 20,000 generated strings are all distinct, support ≥ Ω(M²) = Ω(4×10⁸)
- Model is NOT memorizing if support >> number of model parameters (86M)

**Result 3 — KL Divergence:**
- For each position in each test string, get model's predicted next-token distribution
- Get true CFG's next-token distribution via inside-outside algorithm
- Compute KL(true || model) averaged over all positions and strings

### Key Numbers to Reproduce (Figure 4)

| Dataset | GPT acc | GPT_rel acc | GPT_rot acc | GPT KL | GPT_rel KL |
|---------|---------|-------------|-------------|--------|------------|
| cfg3b | 99.8% | 99.8% | 99.8% | 0.00008 | 0.00011 |
| cfg3g | 64.1% | 99.1% | 98.6% | 0.00450 | 0.00034 |
| cfg3f | 57.1% | 98.8% | 97.6% | 0.00455 | 0.00043 |

Vanilla GPT collapses on hard CFGs. GPT_rel and GPT_rot stay near-perfect.
GPT_uni beats vanilla GPT on hard CFGs — this is a critical finding.

## 5. Results 4-5: Probing Hidden States

### What Is Being Claimed

After pretraining on next-token prediction ONLY (no parse tree supervision), the model's last-layer hidden states linearly encode the complete parse tree — both ancestor symbols s_ℓ(i) and boundary indicators b_ℓ(i) — for every level ℓ and every position i.

### The Multi-Head Linear Probe (Equation 4.2)

To predict s_ℓ(i) (which nonterminal at level ℓ generated token i):

```
G_i(x) = Σ_{r ∈ [H], k ∈ [len(x)]} w_{r,i→k} · f_r(E_k(x))
```

where:
- E_k(x) is the hidden state at position k from the last transformer layer (shape: 768)
- f_r: R^768 → R^3 is a linear map (one per head)
- w_{r,i→k} are attention weights depending ONLY on positions i and k, not on content:
  ```
  w_{r,i→k} = softmax_k( <P_{i,r}, P_{k,r}> )
  ```
  where P_{i,r} ∈ R^{d'} are trainable position embeddings

The whole function G_i(x) is LINEAR in the hidden states E_k(x) — the position-only weights w are fixed for any given (i,k) pair regardless of string content.

**Probe hyperparameters (from Appendix A.4):**
- H = 16 heads
- d' = 1024 (dimension of position embeddings in probe)
- Trained with AdamW: lr=0.003, β=(0.9,0.98), weight decay=0.001
- Batch size 60, 30K iterations
- Fresh samples throughout (never reuse)

### Result 4: Full Probe Accuracy

Using all hidden states {E_k(x)} to predict s_ℓ(i) at every position:

| Dataset | NT6 | NT5 | NT4 | NT3 | NT2 |
|---------|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|
| cfg3b (GPT_rel) | 100% | 100% | 100% | 100% | 100% |
| cfg3f (GPT_rel) | 100% | 97.5% | 94.8% | 92.9% | 93.5% |
| cfg3f (DeBERTa) | 100% | 99.6% | 96.3% | 84.0% | 77.5% |
| cfg3f (GPT_rand) | 71.3% | 49.9% | 44.6% | 59.1% | 68.6% |

GPT_rand (random weights, same architecture) is the critical control — it fails, proving the trained model's high accuracy is a learned property, not an architectural artifact.

### Result 5: Local Probe at NT Boundaries

Restricted probe variants:
- **Diagonal (δ=0):** use only E_i(x) to predict s_ℓ(i)
- **Tridiagonal (δ≤1):** use only E_{i-1}(x), E_i(x), E_{i+1}(x)

These restricted probes are applied ONLY at NT boundary positions where b_ℓ(i)=1.

Result: near-perfect accuracy even with just E_i(x) alone at boundary positions.

| Dataset | NT6 | NT5 | NT4 | NT3 | NT2 |
|---------|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----|
| cfg3b (diagonal, any GPT) | ~100% | ~100% | ~99.6% | ~99.8% | ~100% |
| cfg3f (diagonal, GPT_rel) | ~100% | ~99.2% | ~99.2% | ~98.9% | ~98.4% |

**Interpretation:** boundary positions are "summary positions." By the time the model processes the last token of a subtree, it compresses that subtree's identity into the hidden state at that position. This is exactly where DP(i,j,A) is stored — at position j, the right endpoint.

**Why non-boundary positions cannot do this:** a token in the middle of a subtree has not yet seen how the subtree ends. It is information-theoretically impossible to know the full ancestry of a mid-subtree token from left context alone.

## 6. Results 6-9: Attention Patterns

### Setup: Removing Position Bias

Raw attention: A_{l,h,j→i}(x) = attention weight from j to i at layer l, head h.

Position bias (average attention at distance p):
```
Ā_{l,h,p} = average of A_{l,h,j→i}(x) over all x and pairs (i,j) with j-i=p
```

Residual (position-bias-removed):
```
B_{l,h,j→i}(x) = A_{l,h,j→i}(x) - Ā_{l,h,j-i}
```

B is positive if j attends to i more than distance alone predicts.
B is the content/structure-driven component of attention.

### Result 6: Position-Based Attention

Raw attention is strongly determined by relative distance p = j-i, even for vanilla GPT with absolute embeddings. Different heads attend at different scales (multi-scale): some heads focus on distances 1-10, others on 20-80, others on 100+. This mirrors CFG's multi-scale hierarchical structure.

GPT_pos (position-only attention, no content) achieves ~93-97% on hard CFGs — position alone gets most of the way there.

### Result 7: B Concentrates at NT-End Positions

Average B over pairs (i,j) where i+δ is at an NT boundary (b♯(i+δ)=ℓ):
- Peaks sharply at δ=0 (exactly at boundary)
- Drops rapidly for δ=±1, ±2

NT boundaries attract disproportionate attention from everywhere.

### Result 8: NT-End to NT-End Attention

B is highest when BOTH i and j are NT boundaries at the same level ℓ. Boundary positions preferentially communicate with other boundary positions — they read each other's stored subproblem summaries.

### Result 9: Adjacent NT-End Attention

Define: `B^{end→end}_{l,h,ℓ'→ℓ,r}` = average B over pairs where b♯(i)=ℓ, b♯(j)=ℓ', and p_ℓ(j) - p_ℓ(i) = r (ancestor distance at level ℓ).

This decreases as r increases — attention is highest for r=0 or r=1 (adjacent siblings in parse tree).

**DP interpretation:** to compute DP(i,j,A) by combining DP(i,k,B) and DP(k,j,C), the model at position j reads back to the nearest left boundary k. This is exactly the j→i attention pattern with small r.

## 7. The DP Corollary

Combining all results:

**Storage (Results 4-5):** Hidden states linearly encode DP(i,j,A) values. Specifically, E_j(x) at boundary position j encodes which nonterminal generated the span ending at j.

**Recurrence (Results 7-9):** Position j attends to its nearest left boundary i (small r), reading back the stored DP(i,j,C) to combine with the current computation.

**Two DPs happening simultaneously:**
- Parsing DP: DP(i,j,A) = can A generate tokens x_{i+1}...x_j? (verifying past structure)
- Generative DP₂: DP₂(j,a) = can symbol a follow prefix x_1...x_j? (predicting future tokens)

The model mimics both simultaneously through its attention patterns and hidden state organization.

## 8. Additional Results (Extensions)

### Result 10: Implicit CFGs
Each terminal symbol is a bag of observable tokens (e.g., terminal "noun" → 100 actual words). Models learn implicit CFGs well. Token embeddings from the same bag cluster together — the model encodes hidden terminal identity in the embedding layer.

### Result 11: Robustness
Models trained on clean data achieve only ~30% accuracy on corrupted prefixes (15% tokens randomly flipped). Including 10% corrupted training data boosts robustness to ~80%.

### Result 12: Mode Switch
With mixed training data, model learns two modes: given clean prefix → completes correctly; given corrupted prefix → completes with errors; no prefix → generates corrupted strings with probability γ (the training corruption fraction). Same behavior observed in real LLMs (Llama, GPT-3).

### Result 13: Low Temperature for Robustness
High robust accuracy on corrupted prefixes only achievable at low temperature (τ=0.1-0.2). At τ=1 the model samples from the full distribution including the "error mode."

### Hidden State Extraction

Use PyTorch hooks on the last transformer layer:

```python
def get_last_layer_hidden_states(model, token_ids):
    hidden_states = {}
    
    def hook_fn(module, input, output):
        hidden_states['last'] = output[0].detach().cpu()
    
    # register on last transformer block
    handle = model.transformer.h[-1].register_forward_hook(hook_fn)
    
    with torch.no_grad():
        model(torch.tensor(token_ids).unsqueeze(0))
    
    handle.remove()
    return hidden_states['last'].squeeze(0)  # shape: (seq_len, 768)
```

- Evaluated on fresh strings not seen during probe training

## 11. Common Mistakes to Avoid

**CFG sampler produces wrong boundary labels.** This is the most dangerous bug because everything downstream depends on it. Verify with the unit test in section 9 before proceeding.

**Forgetting binarization.** CYK without binarizing length-3 rules is O(n⁴) and will be extremely slow on 300-token strings.

**Using pretrained GPT-2 weights.** The paper trains from scratch on CFG data with a vocabulary of 5 tokens. Do not load pretrained weights.

**Wrong temperature during evaluation.** Generation accuracy must use multinomial sampling at temperature τ=1 (unmodified distribution). Do not use greedy decoding or beam search.

**Probe training data leakage.** The probe must be trained and evaluated on strings that were never seen during GPT pretraining AND never seen by the other split. Three separate data pools: GPT training, probe training, probe evaluation.

**Not removing position bias before attention analysis.** Results 7-9 are meaningless without subtracting Ā. NT boundaries are also nearby boundaries by default — position and structure are confounded unless you control for distance.

**Probing non-boundary positions for Result 5.** The local probe (diagonal masking) only makes sense AT boundary positions. The paper explicitly conditions on b_ℓ(i)=1.
